Product Case Study

Making Lincoln Center for everyone.

A mobile app concept that removes the friction keeping young, budget-conscious New Yorkers from experiencing the performing arts.

Role
Product Strategy
Type

The Problem

Lincoln Center is invisible to an entire generation.

Young New Yorkers don't skip Lincoln Center because they don't care about the arts. They skip it because every step of the path — from discovery to purchase to arrival — feels like it wasn't designed for them.

A first-hand account

I'm a tech worker and arts lover. Last year I mentioned to a coworker I was seeing The Tales of Hoffmann — he asked if he could come, someone overheard, and ten minutes later I was ordering group tickets for six people who'd never been to the opera.

Young people aren't unwilling. They just need someone to make it easy.

What's actually stopping them

$

"It's going to cost hundreds of dollars"

Price anxiety sets in before anyone even checks. The assumption alone closes the door.

✆

"I'd have to call someone or go in person"

A generation that does everything on a phone draws a hard line at picking up the actual phone.

⇅

"Buying online is still a whole thing"

Which show? Which seats? The ticket websites weren't built for a quick impulse buy on your phone.

01 — Time

Too much friction

Figuring out what's playing, whether it's for you, and how to get there takes real effort. The app must answer "what should I see tonight?" in under 60 seconds.

02 — Money

Feels out of reach

Most young attendees rule Lincoln Center out before even checking prices. We need affordable entry points that feel winnable — not like a consolation prize.

03 — Social

"If my friends go, I'll go"

Young audiences are socially motivated. Without group coordination tools and social proof, even interested users won't show up alone. Going together needs to be easy.

The App

Four screens. One cohesive experience.

The app is built around getting someone from "I'm free tonight" to "I'm going" as fast as possible — with the infrastructure to grow that relationship over time.

Upcoming

The answer to "what's on tonight?" — fast, filtered, and friend-aware. A persistent search bar and scrollable filter pills let users dial in their exact context: budget, vibe, age program, or availability. Saved filter presets mean returning users are one tap from their personalized feed.

  • Tonight's ticket surfaces as a prominent hero card at the top of the feed — never buried
  • Filter by cost, fanciness scale (come as you are → black tie), lottery availability, free events, and exclusive programs like Under 30s and Gala
  • Save custom filter combinations (e.g. "Budget night out") for one-tap access
  • Friend interest indicators appear inline on show cards — social proof without leaving the feed
  • Real Lincoln Center shows: Turandot, Ingrid Michaelson at Geffen, ¡VAYA! festival, El Último Sueño with Under 40s Mixer

Quick Book

Set your preferences — when you're free, how many people, how much you'll spend, what you want to see — and the app finds and books the best match automatically. No browsing, no seat selection, no friction. This replaces the calendar view to prioritize speed and utility.

  • Genuine advantage over website or box office — a 15-minute research task becomes four taps. This experience can't be replicated anywhere else
  • Works with partial resident buy-in — Quick Book functions with whatever inventory is available and expands naturally as more organizations participate
  • Membership-aware — Under 30s discounts, student rates, and lottery eligibility applied automatically
  • Group-friendly by default — buying for 2, 4, or 6 people is built into the flow from step one
  • Matched show surfaces with price and time before final confirmation — one last tap, no surprises

My Tickets

Everything in one place, organized by what you need to act on. Tonight's confirmed tickets are at the top. Active lotteries have visible countdowns. Past shows are archived for re-discovery and review.

  • Three tabs: Upcoming (confirmed tickets), Lotteries (all available lotteries to enter, not just active ones), Past (attended shows)
  • Tonight's tickets always surface first, with a large QR code, directions shortcut, and share button
  • Lottery cards show countdown timer, friend count, and notification ETA — reducing anxiety around the wait
  • Share to Instagram Stories shortcut on the night of a performance
  • Past tab enables users to rate and reflect on shows, feeding the recommendation engine

After the Meeting

Real feedback. Updated proposals.

After sharing this concept with Lincoln Center's team, three concrete concerns came back. Here's what they said — and how the design responds.

📊

Free events go untracked

Lincoln Center runs a significant number of free events — concerts, festivals, open rehearsals — but has no reliable way to capture who attended. Attendance data for these events is essentially invisible.

📲

Why download an app at all?

If users can do the same thing on the website, there's no reason to download an app. The value has to be something the website can't offer — and a ticket wallet alone isn't it. The real advantage is saved preferences: the app knows who you are, what you like, and what you qualify for, so you never have to re-enter it. This matters especially because the website today doesn't support membership-based perks like student tickets or 30 Under 30 pricing — those still require a phone call or an in-person box office visit. The app closes that gap entirely.

🏛

Resident buy-in isn't guaranteed

Lincoln Center's major resident organizations — the Met Opera, NYCB, the Philharmonic — each control their own ticketing. Getting all of them to integrate with a shared app takes time and negotiation.

Two Responses

The design evolves.

Proposal 1 — New Screen

Membership Card & Rewards

A unified digital membership card that lives in the app — scannable at the door for any Lincoln Center event, free or ticketed. All of a user's memberships (Under 30s, student, Young Patrons) consolidate under one credential. Attendance earns points redeemable for coffee, restaurant discounts, and eventually free tickets.

Proposal 1

Membership Card & Rewards

A unified digital membership card — scannable at the door for any Lincoln Center event, free or ticketed. All memberships (Under 30s, student, Young Patrons) consolidate under one credential. Attendance earns points redeemable for coffee, restaurant discounts, and eventually free tickets.

  • Solves free event tracking — users scan their card at the door, giving Lincoln Center the attendance data it currently can't collect for free events

  • Points accrue from any attendance, redeemable for coffee, restaurant discounts, and eventually free tickets
  • Progress bar toward next reward creates a lightweight loop that encourages repeat visits
